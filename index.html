Introduction¶

In this tutorial, we will be analyzing wines from all over the world and learning more about which countries produce the best wine. We will first tidy the data so it will be easier to work with. The data set we are using contains information about each wine's origin, its variety (e.g. Merlot), and its score on Wine Magazine. We will do some exploratory data analysis (EDA) to learn and visualize trends in the data. Is a more expensive wine better than a cheaper one? How much does the age of the wine matter?

The highlight of this tutorial should be a good example of partitioning data to perform piecewise regression.

It will probably be good to know how old the wine is (hint: it gets better with age!). Though our data set doesn't provide it directly, we can extract by deconstructing values in the title column. It is good to note that many wineries have a year in their name (Don Cristobal 1492 for instance), so we'll want to remove the beginning of the title using the winery name availalbe in the winery column. There are also a few points that are missing years and have 'NV' where the year should be, so we'll drop those.

We will add this new column age relative to 2017 to our DataFrame so we can visualize wine getting older in a more natural way.

Exploratory Data Analysis and Visualization¶

With our data tidied, we can start to look for trends.

We'll start with a basic scatter plot of the average price vs. the age of wine over 5 year intervals. We'll guess that the price of wine increases with age.

Hypothesis Testing¶

Qualitatively, our piecewise regression doesn't look too bad! Our R2 still isn't very good at all, but that is expected since the ratings for young wine were all over the place. We've lowered the F critical value (a.k.a. F statistic) to 331.7, and as a result our p-value is many orders smaller. In fact, it's smaller than any reasonable alpha level we could have originally chosen, and for this reason we can confidently reject the null hypothesis. Wine age and rating are probably correlated, and we have a decent model to show it.

It's also important to note that we did not overfit our model. Just for fun, let's try adding an interaction term for the price since we know it reacts similarly to the age:

Here we've clearly overfitted the model. We've included the full set of reviews instead of the 5-year averages in the plot to show how the model attempts to fit every point. However, with only a few variables at play here, the model is too complex to be trusted.

Ways to improve our model¶

We noticed our model improves fits better when we add price as an interaction term. We could also take a look at some insights that we know as wine enthusiasts. For example the variety of wine could help us here. A Champagne is reserved only for sparkling wines from that region, obviously these will cost more. Since we have a lot of data to work with, we could break up prices per variety and see any trends in that direction.

In the beginning of this tutorial, we removed around 20 thousand entries where the price was missing or where the year of wine was not scrapeable. We also did not have much data for wine older than 21 years. We could potentially find compatible datasets for older wines and see if our data changes in any observable way.

Wrap-up¶

From our results, we believe there exists a correlation between the age of a wine and its rating in addition to its price and rating.

It's not surprising to see that price trends upward with increasing age. Older wine is highly sought after. Qualitatively, the data could be fitted with some polynomial or exponential model. It would have been interesting to see how the average price of wine changes after our data set ends. How much more would a 100+ year old wine cost? We could also have looked at how economic factors at the time could have certainly affected prices. A 70+ year old wine corresponds to about the WWII era; are wines found from this period of time (especially ones from European countries) more expensive than say, one from right before 1939 (~80 years old)?

What kind of conclusions can we draw from the rating of a wine vs. its age? Our dataset sample is a factor here: most reviews are for wines no older than 21 years old. Perhaps age is less of a factor for younger wines compared to older ones. If we had more data could potentially see if our square root model continues as the wine gets older.

Looking into the distant future: 50 years from now, how would reviews for wine produced in 2017 compare with 50-year-old reviews for wine produced in 1967?
